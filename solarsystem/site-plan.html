My purpose is to provide educational information about each planet in our solar system. I will use an interactive layout to click through each planet to view its details.

My target audience is students and anyone interested in learning about our solar system. It's designed to be engaging and informative for users of all ages.

Dynamic elements

The website will feature interactive elements such as arrow navigation, dynamic content rendering, planets stored as objects in an arrow, condtional display logic, hover animations, event handling clicks, functions for updating the planet, and image switching.

Home page

The Infinite Frontier

Space has always called to us. Long before telescopes and spacecraft, humans looked up at the night sky and felt the pull of something vast and unknowable — a frontier stretching beyond imagination. Today, that frontier is no longer unreachable. Our solar system, with its eight planets, countless moons, and billions of miles of interplanetary space, is a neighborhood we are only beginning to understand. SolarEd is your portal into that neighborhood — an immersive, data-driven journey through the cosmos built for curious minds who want more than textbook diagrams and static charts.

Planetary Neighbors & Orbital Insights

The planets orbiting our Sun are not distant abstractions. They are active, dynamic worlds — each with its own atmosphere, geology, and story. Saturn's rings are shifting. Jupiter's storms have raged for centuries. Mars hides ancient riverbeds beneath its rust-colored dust. SolarEd brings these worlds to life through orbital data and visualizations, so you can see not just where the planets are, but what elements they contain, and how they formed.

Your Interactive Journey Starts Here

This is not a passive experience. SolarEd invites you to explore at your own pace, dive into the details that fascinate you most, and build a genuine understanding of our place in the universe. Whether you are drawn to the scorching surface of Mercury or the ocean blue color of Neptune, every click takes you deeper into the science. Our solar system is enormous, ancient, and full of surprises — and you have only just arrived at the edge of it. The journey begins now.

Planetary Overview

Mercury

Mercury is the smallest planet in our solar system and the closest to the Sun, yet it is far from the simplest world to understand. Despite its proximity to our star, Mercury is not the hottest planet — its lack of a substantial atmosphere means heat escapes rapidly into space, causing surface temperatures to swing by over 600 degrees Celsius between day and night. The planet's surface is heavily cratered, resembling our own Moon, and bears the scars of billions of years of meteorite impacts. Mercury completes an orbit around the Sun in just 88 Earth days, making its year remarkably short. It was known to ancient civilizations but was first studied in detail by the Mariner 10 spacecraft in 1974.

  • Type: Terrestrial
  • Average Temperature: 167°C
  • Gravity: 0.38g
  • Moons: 0
  • Rings: No

Venus

Venus is often called Earth's twin due to its similar size and mass, but beneath that resemblance lies one of the most hostile environments in the solar system. The planet is blanketed by a thick layer of sulfuric acid clouds that trap heat through a runaway greenhouse effect, making Venus the hottest planet in our solar system despite not being the closest to the Sun. Surface temperatures reach a scorching 465 degrees Celsius — hot enough to melt lead — and atmospheric pressure at the surface is approximately 90 times that of Earth. Venus rotates backwards compared to most planets and does so extraordinarily slowly, meaning a single Venusian day is longer than its year. Spacecraft from both NASA and the Soviet Venera program have revealed a landscape of vast volcanic plains and towering highland regions.

  • Type: Terrestrial
  • Average Temperature: 465°C
  • Gravity: 0.91g
  • Moons: 0
  • Rings: No

Earth

Earth is the only known world in the universe confirmed to harbor life, making it the most scientifically significant planet in our solar system. Its dynamic atmosphere — composed primarily of nitrogen and oxygen — regulates temperature, shields the surface from harmful solar radiation, and drives the weather systems that distribute water across the globe. Earth's surface is roughly 71 percent covered by liquid water, a feature unique among the rocky planets of our solar system. A large, stabilizing Moon helps maintain the axial tilt that gives Earth its predictable seasons. Ongoing volcanic activity, tectonic plate movement, and a powerful magnetic field all contribute to the conditions that make complex life possible here.

  • Type: Terrestrial
  • Average Temperature: 15°C
  • Gravity: 1.00g
  • Moons: 1
  • Rings: No

Mars

Mars, the Red Planet, has captured human imagination for centuries and remains the most explored world beyond Earth. Its distinctive rust-red color comes from iron oxide — essentially rust — covering much of its surface, which also features the tallest volcano in the solar system, Olympus Mons, and a canyon system, Valles Marineris, that dwarfs the Grand Canyon in every dimension. Mars once had a thicker atmosphere and liquid water flowing across its surface, evidence of which remains in ancient dried riverbeds and mineral deposits. Today its thin atmosphere is composed mostly of carbon dioxide, offering little protection from solar radiation or extreme cold. Multiple rovers and landers have explored Mars, and it remains the primary target for future crewed planetary missions.

  • Type: Terrestrial
  • Average Temperature: −60°C
  • Gravity: 0.38g
  • Moons: 2
  • Rings: No

Jupiter

Jupiter is the largest planet in our solar system, so massive that more than 1,300 Earths could fit inside it, and its gravitational influence shapes the orbits of countless other bodies throughout the solar system. Its atmosphere is a turbulent ocean of hydrogen and helium, streaked with bands of ammonia clouds and punctuated by the Great Red Spot — a storm system that has persisted for at least 350 years. Jupiter has no solid surface; pressure and temperature increase with depth until hydrogen is compressed into a metallic, electrically conductive state near the core. Its powerful magnetic field is the strongest of any planet and generates intense radiation belts that pose serious challenges for spacecraft. Jupiter's 95 known moons include Europa, which harbors a subsurface ocean considered one of the most promising candidates for extraterrestrial life.

  • Type: Gas Giant
  • Average Temperature: −110°C
  • Gravity: 2.53g
  • Moons: 101
  • Rings: Yes

Saturn

Saturn is immediately recognizable by its extraordinary ring system — the most extensive and visually dramatic of any planet in the solar system, composed of billions of ice particles and rocky debris ranging from microscopic grains to chunks the size of houses. Like Jupiter, Saturn is a gas giant composed primarily of hydrogen and helium, and it is the least dense planet in our solar system — less dense, in fact, than liquid water. Its atmosphere features powerful wind jets reaching speeds of up to 1,800 kilometers per hour, and its polar regions display unusual hexagonal storm patterns that scientists are still working to fully explain. Saturn's moon Titan is the only moon in the solar system with a dense atmosphere and stable liquid lakes on its surface, though those lakes are filled with liquid methane rather than water. The Cassini spacecraft spent 13 years in orbit around Saturn, transforming our understanding of this remarkable world.

  • Type: Gas Giant
  • Average Temperature: −140°C
  • Gravity: 1.07g
  • Moons: 285
  • Rings: Yes

Uranus

Uranus is an ice giant — a distinct class of planet separate from the gas giants — with an interior rich in water, methane, and ammonia ices surrounding a rocky core. Its most striking characteristic is its extreme axial tilt of approximately 98 degrees, meaning Uranus essentially orbits the Sun on its side, likely the result of a massive collision with an Earth-sized object early in the solar system's history. This tilt causes the most extreme seasons of any planet; each pole experiences roughly 42 years of continuous sunlight followed by 42 years of darkness. Uranus has a faint ring system and 27 known moons, all named after characters from the works of William Shakespeare and Alexander Pope. It was the first planet discovered with a telescope, identified by astronomer William Herschel in 1781.

  • Type: Ice Giant
  • Average Temperature: −195°C
  • Gravity: 0.89g
  • Moons: 28
  • Rings: Yes

Neptune

Neptune is the most distant planet in our solar system and one of the most dynamic, despite receiving only a fraction of the sunlight that reaches the inner planets. Its deep blue color comes from methane in the upper atmosphere absorbing red wavelengths of light, and its winds are the fastest recorded on any planet — reaching speeds of over 2,100 kilometers per hour. Neptune was the first planet whose existence was mathematically predicted before it was directly observed; astronomers Urbain Le Verrier and John Couch Adams independently calculated its position based on gravitational irregularities in Uranus's orbit. Its largest moon, Triton, orbits in the opposite direction of Neptune's rotation — a retrograde orbit suggesting it was captured from the Kuiper Belt rather than forming alongside the planet. Voyager 2 remains the only spacecraft to have visited Neptune, conducting its flyby in 1989 and revealing a complex, storm-driven world at the edge of our solar neighborhood.

  • Type: Ice Giant
  • Average Temperature: −200°C
  • Gravity: 1.14g
  • Moons: 16
  • Rings: Yes
